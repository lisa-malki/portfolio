User Centered Data Visualisation

A selection of design concepts integrating visualisations into everyday interfaces with a focus on usability. Explores principles of human vision and their connection to effective visualisation design.

Project Overview

Invisible bytes of data shape the news we see online, the people we speak to, and the decisions made about us by the powers-that-be. Data is often too big for us to meaningfully comprehend, so must be abstracted: visualisation refers to the practice of encoding and abstracting information using graphical elements.

What we take from a visualisation is the culmination of several design decisions as the same data can be visually encoded in a myriad of different ways. A user-centred approach to visualisation takes into account the context of the end-user, the domain tasks they wish to achieve with a visualisation, and most importantly, their cognitive and perceptive capacities. The best encoding therefore, is that which is the most appropriate for the task at hand, and the user interpreting it.

This project explores some examples of user-centered data visualisation, presenting a fusion of well-known and newer vis idioms in everyday contexts. I use the language of domain tasks and targets to describe the aims of users, and describe the underlying visual principles guiding my choices of encoding.

Smart Thermostat

This mini-project details the process of designing visualisations to be integraded into a domestic smart thermostat.

Domain Tasks

Design decisions were made in relation to the following three domain tasks:

  1. Checking that the smart thermostat is functioning correctly and efficiently: on a basic level, this involves an elementary lookup and comparison, where the target is an outlier, i.e. an instance where the temperature was not recorded or controlled as desired.
  2. Judging how the pattern of heating over time relates to activities in the home: This is a synoptic task , where the target may be any of: trends, outliers, or features.
  3. Checking that the temperature is within a safe range: For this task, the temperature is judged to be either under or over a known baseline, which can be set by the user. Targets include features and extremes/outliers.

This projects uses two data streams for internal and external home temperature. The internal temperature data is a timestamped series of readings in degrees Celsius (samples taken every 10 minutes), with an additional categorical stream for boiler status (ON or OFF). The external temperature dataset has a similar structure and was scraped from WeatherUnderground (link here).

Data Exploration

The tried and tested line chart idiom was chosen to initially visualise the temporal data. Line charts augment dot charts with connecting line marks, creating a very strong implication of trend relationships and continuous flow across points. Line graphs also use less screen real estate than bar charts - an important consideration given the high granularity of the data.

Encoding Categorical Attributes

In addition to showing internal/external temperature, it is also desirable to indicate when the boiler is on and when the internal temperature falls below a defined limit. A full colour overlay was chosen to encode the boiler status. Colour is a relatively effective identity channel, and spatial overlay supports rapid comparison.

Colour hue is additionally used as a visual popout to identify line segments which fall below an example baseline of 20 degrees. Due to parallel processing and the effectiveness of the colour channel in this context, the popout can be perceived even in the presence of other visual distractors.

Interactivity and Context of Use

Interactive features improve the user experience of visualisations at little cost to accuracy or efficiency, and tooltips in particular improve user confidence when navigating time series charts.

Zoom plots embed the time series within a zoomable widget allowing users to customise the timeframe they wish to see. Zooming was implemented using the Plotly Express library, which allows smooth, flexible navigation using a range slider as well as custom zooming on the main plot. Button filters to limit the view to common timeframes (1 month, 3 months, 6 months) are also provided.

Checkbox filters allow the user to select the graph amenities they wish to be visible. This enables them to view only the information that directly supports their task, eliminating distractors, and supporting the process of filtering.

Screen Time Visualiser

Being aware of how long we use our phones and for what purpose is becoming more popular, and many devices already have built-in visualisation displays for screen time, including total time spent, and breakdowns by app. This project will investigate how this data can be most effectively presented, given the constraints of a mobile interface.

Domain Tasks

Design decisions were made in relation to the following three domain tasks:

  1. Identifying the apps, or groups of apps, that take up the most screen time: this is a discovery task, the target of which is the distribution of screen time. Outliers and extremes are of particular interest.
  2. Checking how screen time (overall, or for specific apps) changes over time: this involves discovering temporal trends in the data at different levels of abstraction.

I manually extracted my own app usage data for the period 8th-16th March 2022 from the iOS screen time app. The dataset is a 2D table, with entries representing screen time in minutes indexed by date and app name. Each app is categorized into social, entertainment, information, or other.

Data Exploration: Visualising Temporal Trends

Bar charts facilitate accurate value extraction and efficient comparisons between adjacent (or close-together) bars. This is important, as domain task 2 focuses more on short-term, day-by-day trend comparisons rather than high-level changes over long periods of time. A stacked bar chart is used to show total screen time for each day of the week, disaggregated by app category.

App-Wise Disaggregation

Core to the design is the task of checking screen time for individual apps, which are lower down on the hierarchy than app categories. I initially designed a multiform interface which facets the stacked bar overview with a donut chart, expanding the range of tasks the user can complete peripherally. Each category is shown separately on a tabbed menu screen to reduce visual clutter. Donut charts effectively visualise hierarchical data with the arc length channel rather than the less perceptually salient angle.

A downside of this approach is that users must switch between screens to view different categories, weakening the sense of implicit hierarchy in the data, and increasing cognitive load. Treemaps use subdivided, bounded areas to effectively convey hierarchical structures on displays of limited size without losing attention to detail.

Interactivity and Context of Use

The final design combines the existing pattern of a labelled bar chart with a treemap, to disaggregate by category and app. When the user taps the bar corresponding to a particular day, an app-wise breakdown for that day appears. This facets a weekly overview with a treemap to keep weekly trends in the user's peripheral vision. The display includes descriptive text to support users who need screen-readers to interpret the visualisation

Interactive Exhibit

Datasets have become scientific artefacts, creating a place for museums exhibits which visualise data for a diverse audience. Here, I have designed an interactive museum exhibition visualizing London’s atmospheric emissions and its relationship with borough demographics, allowing users to collaboratively explore data with a personal dimension.

Domain Tasks

  1. Detecting global or local patterns in emissions across London, such as significant north-south or east-west differences in emissions.
  2. Identifying singular values for a chosen geographic location- e.g., viewing the rate of car ownership for one’s own borough.
  3. Searching for known patterns such as clusters and correlations between different demographical and environmental measures

The data for this project includes a GeoJSON file defining borough boundaries, and four additional streams of data for borough atmospheric emissions, population density car ownership, and percentage of green space taken from the GLA borough profiles.

Visualising Geographies

Choropleth Map

Choropleth maps directly convey shape information by spatial elements, and use colour to encode associated ordinal, quantitative or categorical attributes. There is an intuitive concordance between the frequent aggregation of data at regional levels and the use of geometric regions to encode this data. Choropleths also exploit human colour perception through for instance, the dark-is-more bias. This means that even without a legend, high values are implicitly associated with dark colours.

Colourmaps

The choice of colourmap rests on the structure and form of the data. Atmospheric emissions is quantitative, ranging from a (theoretical) minimum of zero, to some maximum. Consequently, making a multi-hue colourmap is inappropriate, as hue, an identity channel, does not carry an inherent message of magnitude.

To better convey perceptual linearity, the luminance channel is used to show progression from a minimum to a maximum with a single-hue, monotonically increasing map. Five colour classes were used to provide sufficient granularity to explore trends without harming the map’s legibility, given the range of the emissions data. The ColorBrewer tool was used to experiment with different colourmaps, and select one that was suitable for colour blind users.

Visualising Relationships

The Humble SPLOM

Scatterplots are particularly effective for visualising correlations between two variables, using perceptual judgements of how well points fall along a diagonal line. The rectilinear layout is familiar to users- an important factor, since museum exhibits are viewed by people of all skill levels.

Scatterplot matrices (SPLOMs) are a good way to lay out multi-dimensional data, and outperform idioms such as parallel plots in correlation identification tasks. They are also more intuitive to novice users, and avoid the clutter caused by overlapping line segments.

As such, relationships between various socio-demographic measures for boroughs in London was visualised using a SPLOM. The Seaborn library was used to implement a SPLOM with point grouping (enclosing points into geographical shapes) to make clusters, correlations, and outliers more visually salient.

Interactivity and Context of Use

Large Scale Display

Large-scale displays reap the benefits of a wide field of view, which exploits peripheral vision to make sense of context while maintaining acuity of the focal area. They also encourage physical, embodied interaction such as walking and head turning, which improves user experience. A larger display which invites shared exploration of data is particularly important in museums, where people collectively make sense of exhibits.

Scatterplot Brushing and Linking

The technique of brushing and linking is implemented on the display, to connect data shown on the map to its corresponding SPLOM representation. When the user taps a borough, it is highlighted, and points corresponding to this borough in the SPLOM are all enlarged and highlighted, so the user can see the matrix-wide behaviour of their borough.
