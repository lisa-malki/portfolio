Redesigning a Church Website

A short UX case study focusing on redesigning the website of a local landmark church to bolster its online presence following lockdown

Project Overview

With the onset of lockdown, many faith communities are turning to the internet to maintain contact and hold services. I redesigned the desktop website of a local landmark church with a particular focus on information architecture and presentation. The site attracts a steady number of new visitors, so as well as catering to the community, it also needs a clear and informative homepage which emphasises key information.

This redesign has been conducted on a voluntary basis over the past few months. I am currently in the process of updating the live St. Mary's website to reflect the design changes, which can be found here. The next stage of the process would be to gather feedback on the UI styling once the UX improvements are actioned.

User Research

Interviews and Observations

To ensure the design was grounded in the needs of users and sensitive to their existing habits, I formulated three core research questions which would guide the process:

  1. What do users generally access the website for? What are their goals?
  2. How easily are users able to complete their goals?
  3. What challenges and pain points do users experience?

To shed some light on these questions, I conducted a remote observation with three regular users of the website, and one user who had never used the site, but had used other church websites. Users were encouraged to think-aloud as they browsed. Design themes were constructed through affinity diagramming, and a user journey map was created to visualise the flow through the website relative to the usersâ€™ goals, emphasising any reported challenges.

It was clear from the analysis that the key usability problems were related to the site's information architecture. More specifically:

  • Regular users primarily use the website to find information about upcoming events.
  • New users instinctively seek out contact details, but struggle to find them.
  • Users miss important information if it is obscured behind unrelated links or dense text.
  • Users are more likely to access information that is visible on the homepage or top level navbar.

Sketching Alternatives

I then analysed a number of existing church websites to understand what users would expect when browsing the site. I inspected five websites, and made notes on the homepage, services, and events page, before identifying patterns which worked well across sites. This served as useful inspiration for design alternatives for the homepage and services page, which were rapidly sketched.

Homepage Macrostructures

I developed three core concepts for an improved homepage, focusing on the high-level page architecture. I experimented with vertical and horizontal navigational menus, and different spatial layouts for page elements.

The key design aim was to make the site contact details more visually prominent, and offer an appropriate preview of the church's services and events while reducing visual clutter.

Homepage Events Preview

I also sketched some design alternatives for previewing church events on the homepage, experimenting with different UI patterns to convey information.

Service Page Redesign

Having sketched design alternatives for the homepage, I wireframed a new services page. I converged on two alternatives with varying calendar layouts and methods for presenting service livestreams-a new addition to the site.

Medium-Fidelity Prototyping

Home and Services Page

I selected the strongest concepts to wireframe in medium-fidelity using Adobe XD. Designs which prompted visibility of information and flexibility were prioritised, allowing me to narrow the design space to two homepage alternatives, a services page, and a sermons page.

I then conducted remote usability tests to compare the two designs. Users were sent a link to the interactive prototype, and observed over zoom as they navigated the site. Each participant was invited to browse both versions (A then B) before completing a brief post-task interview. I inferred usability insights from their observed actions, their stated points of frustration, and overall impressions.

Key observations included:

  • Users rarely scrolled to the footer before navigating away from the homepage, suggesting that putting contact details in the footer might not be valuable.
  • When watching videos on YouTube, users remarked that they liked to peripherally browse content on the page around and underneath the video playerâ€“ making a modal unsuitable for sermon livestreams.
  • Homepage A was perceived to be easier to use, less cluttered, and more informative than B across participants.

Generally, users preferred homepage A over B, and were better able to navigate to key pages such as services and contact on the former. It was also decided that embedding videos in a separate page rather than appearing as a modal overlay would better support peripheral browsing.

Church Blog

In addition to the home and services page, I also developed a medium-fidelity wireframe for the chuch blog page, and am in the process of evaluating the whole site with users.

Outcomes

The redesign has improved the information architecture, usability, and aesthetic appeal of the site. Content is visible and organised in a way that is logical, and intuitive to find. I grounded my design criteria in the needs of users, and conducted research into existing church websites to develop a UI vocabulary which was familiar to users.

I am currently implementing the new features and designs on the live site, and will soon focus my attention on the mobile UI. This will involve a dedicated sketching and prototyping session, as the X can be affected significantly by the size of the viewport.
